\section{Results}

\subsection {Model Training}

Hyperparameters for all models were selected with Optuna using a separate study for each model and a median pruner that terminates weak trials early.
The best configurations were then trained for 100 epochs with cosine annealing and early stopping.
The final validation losses are reported in Table~\ref{tab:training}.

When interpreting these results, one needs to bear in mind that pixel models minimize pixel-space MSE whereas JEPA models minimize an embedding-space MSE with VICReg regularization.
Hence, values for both approaches are not directly comparable with each other.

\label{tab:training}
\begin{tabular}{llll}

Model & Validation loss & Loss space & Epochs \\
\midrule
Pixel-CNN & 0.019 & pixel MSE & 100 \\
Pixel-ViT & 0.022 & pixel MSE & 100 \\
Patch-JEPA & 0.013 & embedding MSE + VICReg & 100 \\
PatchDecoder & 0.00085 & pixel MSE (next frame) & 100 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\smallskip
\raggedright\small\textit{Note.} MSE = mean squared error; VICReg = variance and covariance regularization.
\end{table}


For Patch-JEPA, a separate PatchDecoder was trained on the frozen encoder and predictor to map predicted patch embeddings back to pixel space (validation loss 0.000845, next-frame pixel MSE).
All quantitative comparisons in the following subsections are therefore carried out in pixel space.

\subsection {Evaluation}

We evaluate Pixel-CNN, Pixel-ViT, and Patch-JEPA in three scenarios: one-step prediction accuracy, multi-step autoregressive rollout, and robustness to input noise.
All evaluations use held-out validation trajectories in which the organism remains active throughout the evaluation window.
This filter is essential, since every model predicts a black frame on trajectories with dying organisms, inflating the apparent performance of the weakest models.

\paragraph{One-step prediction.} Table~\ref{tab:onestep} shows one-step prediction results on 2000 held-out frame pairs, together with the rollout competence horizon defined below.
In addition to the mean squared error (MSE), we report two standard image-quality
metrics.
The peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR, in decibels) expresses the ratio between the maximum possible pixel value and the prediction error on a logarithmic scale.
Higher values indicate a more accurate reconstruction.
The structural similarity index (SSIM) compares local luminance, contrast, and structure between prediction and ground truth, values ranging from 0 to 1, where 1 corresponds to identical images.
Unlike MSE, it is designed to reflect perceived structural similarity rather than per-pixel differences.
Patch-JEPA achieves the best scores on all three metrics.
However, these numbers must be interpreted with caution, since consecutive Lenia frames are highly similar and predicting no change at all already achieves a low error (identity baseline MSE of 0.00045).
Strong one-step scores can thus be reached without capturing the underlying dynamics.

\paragraph{Multi-step rollout.} Each model was rolled out autoregressively for 30 steps from the same initial frames, averaged over five active validation trajectories (Figure~\ref{fig:rollout}).
We compare against the identity baseline and define the competence horizon as the last step at which a model remains below this baseline.
Patch-JEPA achieves the lowest step-one error (0.00173, roughly half the baseline of 0.00333) and the longest competence horizon (step 10), followed by Pixel-ViT (step 8).
Pixel-CNN exceeds the baseline already at step one, meaning that predicting no change would outperform it at every horizon on active trajectories.
Beyond step 10 all three models exceed the identity baseline.

\paragraph{Robustness to input noise.}
Adding Gaussian noise to the input frame degrades all models.
In relative terms Patch-JEPA degrades most strongly (+768\% one-step MSE at $\sigma=0.2$ versus roughly +200\% to +260\% for the pixel models), but this percentage is misleading because its clean-input error is far lower.
In absolute terms Patch-JEPA remains the most accurate model at every noise level, with an MSE of 0.0134 at $\sigma=0.2$ compared to 0.027-0.029 for both pixel models.

\subsection{Interventions}
Table~\ref{tab:interventions} reports the post-intervention MSE at step ten.
Patch-JEPA achieves the lowest error in four of the five interventions, while Pixel-CNN is the weakest in four of five (Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap}).
The global \texttt{scale\_density} perturbation is the hardest case for all models and the only one in which Pixel-ViT is best.
After \texttt{zero\_region}, all models predict that surrounding live cells regrow into the killed area within one to two steps, matching the ground-truth simulation.
